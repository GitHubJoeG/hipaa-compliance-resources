Telehealth and HIPAA Compliance: What You Need to Know

The pandemic accelerated telehealth adoption across healthcare, and the regulatory landscape has evolved accordingly. While the HHS Office for Civil Rights granted enforcement discretion for certain telehealth platforms during COVID-19, that discretion ended in March 2023. Today, all telehealth services must fully comply with HIPAA requirements, with no exceptions for consumer platforms.

Telehealth-specific compliance differs from traditional in-person practice in meaningful ways. Video conferencing adds technical safeguard requirements. Remote provider locations introduce physical security challenges. Asynchronous communication opens new privacy considerations. Integrating telehealth systems with existing EHR platforms creates vendor management complexity.

The Core Principle Whether your patient is sitting in your office or joining via video from home, HIPAA compliance requirements remain identical. Any platform you use to deliver care or discuss patient health information must protect that information with the same rigor as you would in a traditional practice.

This guide walks you through every aspect of telehealth HIPAA compliance, from platform selection to managing risk, so you can confidently expand virtual care while protecting patient privacy and your organization's compliance posture.

Post-Pandemic Telehealth Enforcement (2024-2026)

Enforcement Discretion Has Ended

During the COVID-19 pandemic, the HHS Office for Civil Rights issued Notification of Enforcement Discretion (March 2020) allowing covered entities to use non-compliant platforms under emergency circumstances. This discretion expired on December 31, 2024. As of 2025, all telehealth platforms must fully meet HIPAA requirements with no exceptions.

Critical Change for Your Practice If you've been using platforms like regular Zoom, Google Meet, Skype, or WhatsApp for telehealth, you must immediately transition to HIPAA-compliant alternatives. Continuing to use non-compliant platforms exposes your practice to significant OCR enforcement risk and potential penalties.

OCR Focus Areas in Telehealth

Recent OCR enforcement actions and audit priorities in telehealth compliance include:

  • Use of non-compliant video platforms for clinical consultations or patient care
  • Missing or inadequate Business Associate Agreements with telehealth platform vendors
  • Inadequate patient consent processes for telehealth visits and recordings
  • Lack of documentation regarding security risk assessment for telehealth systems
  • Unsecured transmission of PHI through email, text, or consumer messaging apps
  • Inadequate encryption of video recordings and stored telehealth data
  • Failure to assess telehealth-specific risks during Security Risk Analysis
  • Inadequate access controls and audit logging for telehealth platforms
Enforce Internally Now Rather than waiting for OCR contact, conduct your own telehealth compliance audit. Review what platforms your providers are using, verify BAAs are signed, update your Security Risk Analysis to address telehealth specifically, and document your compliance efforts. This demonstrates good faith compliance and reduces your exposure significantly.

HIPAA-Compliant Telehealth Platforms vs. Consumer Tools

Why Consumer Platforms Don't Work

Regular Zoom, Google Meet, Microsoft Teams (standard edition), Apple FaceTime, Skype, WhatsApp, and similar consumer video conferencing tools are not HIPAA-compliant, even if they offer encryption. These platforms lack the required Business Associate Agreement, don't meet HIPAA's security standards, and their privacy policies allow for uses of data (like training algorithms or analytics) that violate HIPAA's minimum necessary standard.

Encryption Requirements for Telehealth

Encryption Standards Under HIPAA

The HIPAA Security Rule requires encryption of electronic PHI transmitted over networks. For telehealth, this applies to the video call itself, the audio stream, any chat messages, screen shares, and recordings.

In-Transit Encryption (TLS 1.2+)

All telehealth platforms must use Transport Layer Security (TLS) version 1.2 or higher to encrypt data as it travels across the internet. This is now the minimum standard across the industry. TLS 1.0 and 1.1 have been deprecated and are no longer acceptable. Your platform vendor should document their TLS version in security documentation or their BAA.

End-to-End Encryption (E2EE)

End-to-end encryption is considered the gold standard for telehealth. With E2EE, the telehealth platform encrypts the video and audio at the provider's end and decrypts it only on the patient's device (or vice versa). The platform itself never has access to unencrypted call content. This means even if the platform's servers were compromised, attackers could not view the call.

Many HIPAA-compliant platforms offer E2EE as an optional feature. When available, it's worth enabling, though the standard TLS encryption is sufficient for HIPAA compliance. Document your encryption choices in your Security Risk Analysis.

Encryption at Rest

Telehealth recordings, transcripts, chat logs, and any stored data must be encrypted at rest (stored in encrypted form on servers). Your BAA should specify the encryption standard used. AES-256 is considered industry best practice.

Device-Level Encryption

Ensure your provider devices (computers, tablets, phones) are encrypted at the device level using full-disk encryption (FileVault on Mac, BitLocker on Windows, or native device encryption on mobile devices). This protects PHI if a device is lost or stolen.

Unencrypted Wi-Fi Risks If a provider joins a telehealth call over unencrypted public Wi-Fi (like at a coffee shop), the connection should still be protected by the platform's TLS encryption. However, your policy should discourage providers from using unencrypted public networks when possible, and require VPN use if unencrypted networks must be used.

Business Associate Agreements with Telehealth Vendors

Why BAAs Are Essential

Under HIPAA, you cannot share PHI with any vendor without a signed Business Associate Agreement in place. Telehealth platforms are clearly business associates because they have access to patient health information. Using a telehealth platform without a BAA is a direct HIPAA violation, regardless of how secure the platform is.

What to Verify in Your Telehealth BAA

When signing or reviewing a BAA with your telehealth vendor, ensure it includes:

  • Permitted Uses: BAA clearly limits the vendor to using PHI only to provide telehealth services on your behalf
  • Safeguards: Vendor commits to implementing administrative, physical, and technical safeguards consistent with HIPAA requirements
  • Encryption: Specific commitment to encrypt ePHI in transit and at rest, with documented standards (TLS 1.2+, AES-256, etc.)
  • Breach Notification: Vendor commits to notify you of any known security incident involving PHI within a defined timeframe (typically 24-48 hours)
  • Subcontractors: Vendor ensures any subcontractors also sign BAAs covering the same requirements
  • Audit Rights: You retain the right to audit the vendor's security practices and conduct security assessments
  • Data Return/Destruction: Vendor commits to return or securely destroy all PHI upon termination of your agreement
  • Termination Obligations: Clear process for how data will be handled if you stop using the platform
  • Data Location: If relevant, BAA specifies where data is stored and processed (U.S. only vs. international hosting)
  • Liability Provisions: Defines indemnification and liability if the vendor breaches the BAA

HHS Sample BAA Provisions

The HHS provides sample BAA provisions that serve as excellent templates. Many vendors will already incorporate these standard provisions. If a vendor pushes back on including specific safeguards in their BAA, that's a red flag. A reputable telehealth platform will have a standard BAA that covers all required HIPAA safeguards.

Don't Assume Cloud Hosting is HIPAA-Compliant Using a HIPAA-compliant cloud hosting service (like AWS HIPAA Eligible Services or Microsoft Azure HIPAA) does not automatically make your telehealth setup compliant. You still need a BAA with the platform itself, your own implementation of appropriate safeguards, and documented risk management practices.

Managing Multiple Vendor BAAs

Create a vendor BAA spreadsheet tracking: vendor name, BAA execution date, renewal date, key contact, platform features covered, and any outstanding items. This helps ensure you never have a gap in coverage when vendors don't renew BAAs on time.

Patient Consent for Telehealth

Informed Consent for Virtual Care

Before conducting any telehealth visit, you must obtain explicit informed consent from the patient acknowledging they understand the telehealth visit is not the same as in-person care and they accept the associated risks and benefits. This consent should be documented in the patient's chart.

Key Elements of Telehealth Consent

Your telehealth consent form should address:

  • Nature of Telehealth: Explain that the patient will interact with the provider remotely via video and/or audio, not in-person
  • Technology Risks: Disclose that technology can fail (connection drops, audio/video issues) that might require rescheduling
  • Privacy Limitations: Explain limitations of privacy in telehealth (patients on unsecured networks, in shared spaces, etc.)
  • Right to In-Person Care: Confirm the patient understands they can request in-person evaluation if needed
  • Record-Keeping: Explain how the telehealth visit will be documented and what information will be in the medical record
  • Recording Consent (if applicable): If you record telehealth visits for quality assurance or training, explicitly disclose this and get separate consent
  • Emergency Limitations: Explain that telehealth cannot handle true emergencies and direct patients to call 911 if needed
  • Platform Security: Explain that the telehealth platform uses encryption and complies with privacy laws
  • State Licensing: Confirm the provider is licensed in the patient's state (critical for telehealth across state lines)

Recording Consent

If you record any telehealth visit, you must have explicit, separate consent from all parties in the call (provider and patient, and any other family members or clinicians participating). Simply stating "this call may be recorded" is not sufficient—you need affirmative consent. Document consent in the patient's chart with the date and who consented.

Recording State Law Variations Some states (like California and Florida) require "all-party consent" for recording, meaning all participants must consent before recording begins. Others allow "one-party consent." Always check your state's recording laws before implementing a telehealth recording program. When in doubt, obtain consent from all parties.

Ongoing Consent

Obtain consent for telehealth during the patient's first virtual visit or, at minimum, before their first telehealth appointment. Annual renewal of consent is considered best practice, though not strictly required. If you make significant changes to your telehealth platform or practices (changing to a new video platform, starting to record visits, etc.), re-obtain consent.

Consent is Not Optional Some providers think consent is implied if the patient shows up to a scheduled telehealth appointment. It's not. Conduct the consent process before the visit begins. Many HIPAA-compliant platforms include automated consent flows that ask the patient to confirm their consent as part of joining the call—leverage these built-in features.

Provider Environment Requirements for Remote Care

Physical Security of Remote Spaces

When providers deliver telehealth from home or other remote locations, you must ensure the environment has appropriate physical security to protect patient privacy:

  • Privacy and Interruptions: Provider must be in a location where conversations cannot be overheard by others, with the door closed if in a shared space
  • Screen Privacy: Provider's screen should not be visible to others in the space; position monitors to face away from windows or shared areas
  • Minimal Background: Providers should use virtual backgrounds or ensure their physical background is professional and doesn't disclose unnecessary clinical information
  • No PHI Visible: Ensure patient charts, notes, or other documents containing PHI are not visible on the provider's desk or in the camera background
  • Household Awareness: Brief other household members not to interrupt calls or speak in the background during patient consultations
  • Safe Surroundings: Environment should be reasonably secure from unauthorized access; locked office or residential space is preferable to public locations

Telehealth Workspace Policy

Create a written telehealth workspace policy that all remote providers must follow. The policy should be part of your HIPAA Security Rule documentation and your Security Risk Analysis. Have providers acknowledge the policy and attest that their remote workspace meets the requirements before they begin delivering telehealth.

Acceptable Locations for Telehealth

Different environments carry different privacy risks:

  • Home Office (Best): Private dedicated space, lockable door, provider has full control
  • Urgent Care/Branch Office: Organization-controlled space with appropriate access controls
  • Shared Office Space (Careful): If using shared office, ensure privacy during calls and that others don't have access to clinical systems
  • Public Locations (Avoid): Coffee shops, restaurants, airports, libraries are generally unacceptable for clinical telehealth calls due to privacy and security risks
  • Vehicles (Avoid): Telemedicine from a car should be avoided; not a secure or private environment
  • Patient's Home (Special Considerations): If telehealth is conducted from the patient's location (the provider calls into the patient's home), document this decision and ensure the patient is consented
Hybrid Work and Physical Audits If your organization conducts remote work monitoring or home workspace audits for telehealth providers, document these audits in your compliance program. OCR may inquire about this during investigations. Photos of compliant workspaces, signed attestations, and periodic re-verification demonstrate an organized compliance approach.

Device Security for Remote Providers

Device Requirements

Any device used by a provider to deliver telehealth or access patient information must meet HIPAA security requirements:

  • Device Encryption: Full-disk encryption enabled on all provider devices (Mac, Windows, iOS, Android)
  • Automatic Locking: Devices must lock after 5-10 minutes of inactivity
  • Strong Passwords: Enforce complex passwords (minimum 12-16 characters with upper, lower, numbers, symbols)
  • Multifactor Authentication (MFA): All devices accessing clinical systems should require MFA, especially for EHR login
  • OS and Software Updates: Require automatic updates for operating systems, browsers, and applications
  • Antivirus/Antimalware: All devices must have current endpoint protection software installed and running
  • Firewall Enabled: Windows Firewall, macOS firewall, or mobile OS security features must be active
  • No Personal Apps for Clinical Use: Personal email, cloud storage, messaging apps, etc. should not be used for any clinical communication or PHI transmission

Device Ownership: Bring-Your-Own-Device (BYOD)

If your organization allows providers to use personally-owned devices for telehealth (BYOD), you must have a written policy covering:

  • Device requirements (encryption, security software, minimum OS version)
  • MFA requirements
  • Your organization's right to audit/verify device compliance
  • Mobile device management (MDM) enrollment if using smartphones or tablets
  • What happens to organizational data if the provider leaves or device is lost/stolen
  • Employee responsibility for maintaining device security
  • Permission to wipe organizational data remotely if needed

Organization-issued devices are preferable to BYOD because you have more control over security configuration and updates. However, BYOD can be compliant if you enforce strong policies and MDM enrollment.

Unpatched or Legacy Devices

Older devices running unsupported operating systems (e.g., Windows 7, macOS 10.12) should not be used for telehealth. Do not allow providers to use devices that are no longer receiving security updates from the manufacturer. This is a common finding in OCR investigations and represents a significant security risk.

Mobile Device Management (MDM) If your providers use mobile devices (iPhone, iPad, Android phones) for telehealth, strongly consider MDM enrollment. MDM allows you to enforce security policies, require MFA, enforce encryption, remotely wipe devices if lost, and audit device compliance. This significantly strengthens your security posture and demonstrates intentional compliance.

USB Devices and External Storage

Discourage or prohibit the use of USB drives, external hard drives, or portable storage devices for storing or transferring patient information. If these devices must be used, they must be encrypted. Better practice is to use your secure EHR system for all PHI storage and transmission rather than portable devices.

Recording and Documentation Compliance

Recording Telehealth Visits

While you have the right to record telehealth visits for clinical documentation, quality assurance, or training purposes, you must have explicit prior consent from all parties. The consent must be separate from general telehealth consent and documented in the patient's chart.

Recording Best Practices

  • Explicit Consent: Obtain written consent before the visit begins, not after
  • Document Consent: Record who consented, when, and what was consented to in the chart
  • Notify Participants: Some platforms display a notice when recording begins—use this feature to remind participants
  • Storage and Encryption: Store recordings encrypted with AES-256 or higher on secure servers with access controls
  • Retention Policy: Define how long recordings will be kept before deletion (typically 30 days to 2 years depending on clinical need)
  • Access Controls: Restrict who can access recordings—typically only the provider and clinical quality team, not all staff
  • Audit Logging: Track who accessed each recording and when for security audit purposes
  • Third-Party Disclosure: Never share recordings with anyone outside your organization without separate patient consent

Automatic Transcription and AI

Some platforms offer automatic transcription of telehealth calls using AI. These services have significant privacy and security implications:

  • Obtain explicit consent for transcription before the visit
  • Ensure the transcription vendor has a signed BAA
  • Transcriptions are PHI and must be encrypted, stored securely, and treated like medical records
  • If AI-generated transcripts have errors, don't simply store them—validate accuracy before filing
  • Clarify who can access transcripts and for what purposes

Documentation Standards

Telehealth visit documentation should follow the same standards as in-person visits:

  • Document the date, time, and type of telehealth visit (video, phone, asynchronous messaging, etc.)
  • Note technical issues (if the call dropped or audio/video problems occurred)
  • Document patient's location (if relevant for quality or compliance purposes)
  • Include assessment and plan just as you would for in-person visit
  • Note any communication limitations (e.g., patient in noisy environment, unable to examine physically)
Phone-Only Telehealth Not all telehealth must be video-based. Phone-only telehealth is compliant if it's appropriate for the clinical situation. Documentation for phone visits should note the medium and any limitations. However, be aware that many telehealth platforms don't include phone calls in their security scope—if using phone, verify it's through your HIPAA-compliant platform or use a company phone line, not personal cell phones for clinical calls.

State-Specific Telehealth Regulations

Telehealth Across State Lines

HIPAA is federal law, but telehealth is also regulated at the state level. If your organization serves patients in multiple states, you must comply with each state's telehealth requirements in addition to HIPAA. Key variables include:

  • Licensure Requirements: Providers must be licensed in the state where the patient is located (not where the provider is located). Some states have reciprocity agreements; others don't.
  • Initial In-Person Visit: Some states require at least one in-person visit before telehealth can be provided; others have no requirement
  • Prescription Authority: Some states restrict prescription medications via telehealth; others have no restrictions
  • Recording Laws: As mentioned, some states require all-party consent for recording; others allow one-party consent
  • Documentation Standards: Some states have specific requirements for how telehealth visits must be documented
  • Platform Requirements: A few states explicitly require certain security standards for platforms

Post-Pandemic Telehealth Regulations (2024-2026)

Many states relaxed telehealth requirements during COVID-19, but these are being normalized. Key trends include:

  • Most states now allow audio-only telehealth for appropriate clinical situations
  • Initial in-person visit requirements have been largely suspended
  • Licensure reciprocity varies—multi-state medical licenses are becoming more common
  • Drug testing and prescription monitoring program (PMP) checks still required for controlled substance prescriptions
  • Some states are implementing specific telehealth security regulations beyond HIPAA

States with Specific Telehealth Security Requirements

A few states have enacted their own healthcare data security laws (beyond HIPAA) that may apply to telehealth:

  • California: California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) applies to health data; some healthcare-specific provisions beyond HIPAA
  • New York: NY SHIELD Act requires notification of breaches; 23 NYCRR 500 establishes cybersecurity requirements
  • Massachusetts: Massachusetts Standards for Protecting Personal Information (201 CMR 17.00) require encryption and strong administrative controls
  • Colorado: Colorado Privacy Act (CPA) provides privacy rights to consumers
Research Your States Visit each state's health department or telehealth-specific resource page to verify current requirements. State regulations change frequently, and what was true last year might be different today. Document your compliance with state-specific requirements in your policies and your Security Risk Analysis.

Telehealth-Specific Risk Assessment Considerations

Security Risk Analysis for Telehealth

Your organization's Security Risk Analysis must specifically address telehealth, not just general IT systems. When updating your SRA to include telehealth, consider unique risks:

Telehealth-Specific Threats

  • Platform Compromise: What if the telehealth platform itself is breached? Assess the vendor's security track record, their breach response capabilities, and the scope of data they have access to
  • Insecure Provider Locations: Remote providers in home offices, shared spaces, or public locations introduce physical security risks not present in your office
  • Unmanaged Provider Devices: If providers use BYOD, assess the difficulty of enforcing security controls vs. organization-issued devices
  • Network Interception: Assess the risk of network attacks on unencrypted or poorly encrypted telehealth connections, especially for providers on public Wi-Fi
  • Patient-Side Risks: While your responsibility is limited, assess the risk that patients join calls on unsecured devices or networks, potentially compromising privacy
  • Recording and Transcription Risks: If you record calls or use AI transcription, assess the security of storage, access controls, and vendor management
  • Asynchronous Communication: If you offer secure messaging or asynchronous video, assess storage security, retention policies, and access controls
  • EHR Integration: If your telehealth platform integrates with EHR, assess the security of that integration and API vulnerability
  • Virtual Background and Screen Sharing: Assess risks of providers accidentally sharing PHI through screen sharing or revealing clinical information in backgrounds

Risk Mitigation for Telehealth

After identifying risks, document mitigation strategies:

  • Select HIPAA-compliant platforms with proven security track records
  • Ensure all vendors have signed BAAs with comprehensive security requirements
  • Implement device security policies (encryption, MFA, automatic locking)
  • Provide staff training on telehealth security (avoiding public Wi-Fi, securing workspaces, managing backgrounds)
  • Implement access controls and audit logging in telehealth platforms
  • Conduct regular security assessments of telehealth infrastructure
  • Document incident response procedures specific to telehealth breaches
  • Establish vendor management procedures with periodic security assessments
Update Your SRA Now If your last Security Risk Analysis was conducted before 2023 or doesn't specifically address telehealth, you need an updated assessment. OCR auditors will ask about telehealth in your SRA. Having a current, telehealth-specific SRA with documented risk identification and mitigation demonstrates compliance commitment.

Patient Authentication and Identity Verification

Verifying Patient Identity in Telehealth

Before beginning a telehealth visit, you must verify you're speaking with the correct patient. This is more challenging remotely than in-person (where you compare ID to face). Implement a reliable patient authentication process:

  • Pre-Visit Verification: Confirm name, date of birth, and last four digits of SSN or medical record number before the visit begins
  • Email Confirmation: Send appointment confirmation to patient's verified email address
  • Phone Call Option: Have patient call the clinic at a known number to confirm appointment details
  • Personal Question: Ask a security question only the patient would know ("What street did you grow up on?" or reference previous visit details)
  • Multi-Factor Verification: For sensitive visits, use multiple verification methods

Provider Identification

Just as you identify the patient, the patient should be able to verify they're speaking with the correct provider. Your telehealth platform should display the provider's name, photo, and credentials at the start of the call.

Special Considerations for Minors and Guardians

For telehealth with pediatric or mental health patients, verify that appropriate guardians are present and consenting to the telehealth visit. Document who is in the room during the telehealth visit for minors.

Identity Theft and Fraud Prevention

Consider the risk that fraudsters may attempt to schedule telehealth visits using stolen identities to obtain controlled substances or sensitive treatment. Implement authentication measures that are stringent enough to reduce this risk, especially for specialty telehealth (psychiatry prescribing controlled substances, addiction medicine, etc.).

Telehealth Fraud is Real There have been cases of criminals scheduling telehealth appointments under false identities to obtain controlled substances. If you prescribe medications via telehealth, especially controlled substances, invest in stronger identity verification (photo ID verification, facial recognition, or verification calls to known phone numbers).

Integrating Telehealth with EHR Systems Securely

EHR-Telehealth Integration Risks

Many practices integrate their telehealth platform with their EHR system to streamline workflows. This integration creates security risks if not implemented properly:

  • API Security: The connection between telehealth platform and EHR uses APIs (application programming interfaces). These APIs must be secured with encryption and access controls
  • Authentication: The telehealth platform must authenticate to your EHR with strong credentials (not hardcoded passwords)
  • Data Scope: Limit the data the telehealth platform can access to only what's necessary for the patient visit
  • Audit Logging: All data accessed from EHR via the telehealth platform should be logged for audit
  • Vendor Dependencies: If the telehealth platform goes down, can your EHR still function and vice versa?

Secure Integration Architecture

Work with your EHR vendor and telehealth vendor to ensure secure integration:

  • APIs use OAuth 2.0 or modern authentication protocols (not basic auth)
  • All API communication is over HTTPS/TLS 1.2+
  • Rate limiting and access controls prevent data exfiltration
  • Telehealth platform can only read/write the specific data fields needed for patient care
  • Integration is regularly tested for security vulnerabilities
  • Both vendors have BAAs or integration agreements specifying security responsibilities

Single Sign-On (SSO) Considerations

Many practices implement SSO (single sign-on) so providers can log into telehealth using their EHR credentials. This is convenient, but ensure:

  • SSO connection uses secure protocols (SAML 2.0 or OIDC, not LDAP over unencrypted networks)
  • Session management is secure (automatic logoff after inactivity, session timeout)
  • Failed login attempts are logged and monitored for brute force attacks
  • Your EHR and telehealth platform share consistent MFA requirements

Data Synchronization and Backup

If your telehealth system stores copies of patient data (visit notes, recordings, etc.) separately from your main EHR, you must ensure:

  • Data synchronization is secure and encrypted
  • Backup and disaster recovery processes include telehealth data
  • Retention and deletion policies are consistent between systems
  • Audit trails are maintained for data movement between systems
Get Integration Documentation Request detailed security documentation from both your telehealth and EHR vendors describing their integration architecture, API security, and how data is protected. This documentation should be reviewed and incorporated into your Security Risk Analysis. Don't assume integration is secure just because both vendors are HIPAA-compliant—integration adds new attack surfaces.

Common Telehealth HIPAA Violations and How to Avoid Them

Violation #1: Using Non-Compliant Platforms

The Problem: Using regular Zoom, Google Meet, Microsoft Teams, FaceTime, Skype, or WhatsApp for telehealth visits containing PHI.

Why It's a Violation: These platforms lack Business Associate Agreements, don't meet HIPAA security standards, and their use exposes PHI to unauthorized access. The OCR issued clear guidance: these platforms are not acceptable for PHI.

How to Avoid It: Switch immediately to HIPAA-compliant platforms like Zoom for Healthcare, Doxy.me, or others listed in this guide. Audit all your providers to ensure they're not using consumer platforms. Have IT block access to consumer video platforms from corporate networks if possible.

Violation #2: Missing Business Associate Agreements

The Problem: Using a telehealth platform without a signed BAA in place.

Why It's a Violation: HIPAA explicitly prohibits sharing PHI with vendors without a BAA. This is a foundational HIPAA requirement. Recent OCR enforcement has been aggressive on BAA violations.

How to Avoid It: Before deploying any telehealth platform, request a BAA from the vendor. Many have standard BAAs ready to sign. Document that the BAA has been executed. Track BAA renewal dates and follow up with vendors 60 days before expiration.

Violation #3: Inadequate Encryption

The Problem: Video calls transmitted without encryption or using outdated encryption standards (TLS 1.0 or 1.1).

Why It's a Violation: HIPAA requires ePHI to be encrypted in transit and at rest. Using weak or outdated encryption doesn't meet this requirement.

How to Avoid It: Verify your telehealth platform uses TLS 1.2 or higher. Request documentation from the vendor. Test your platform's encryption using tools like SSL Labs. For sensitive consultations, use platforms that offer end-to-end encryption.

Violation #4: Lack of Patient Consent

The Problem: Conducting telehealth without documented patient consent or without disclosing recording.

Why It's a Violation: HIPAA requires informed consent for telehealth. If you record, you need separate explicit consent. Not obtaining consent is a Privacy Rule violation.

How to Avoid It: Develop a telehealth consent form covering all required elements (nature of telehealth, risks, benefits, recording if applicable). Have patients sign before their first visit. If you change your practices (new platform, now recording, etc.), re-obtain consent.

Violation #5: Insecure Provider Environments

The Problem: Providers conducting telehealth from spaces lacking privacy (open offices, public areas, vehicles) without appropriate safeguards.

Why It's a Violation: The Security Rule requires physical safeguards for PHI. Conducting clinical calls in public spaces violates physical access controls and minimum necessary principle.

How to Avoid It: Establish a remote workspace policy requiring privacy (closed doors, private areas). Have providers attest to workspace compliance. Conduct surprise spot checks or periodic audits. Prohibit public-location telehealth except in emergencies, and document any exceptions.

Violation #6: No Audit Logging

The Problem: Using a telehealth platform that doesn't log who accessed what data and when.

Why It's a Violation: HIPAA's Security Rule requires audit logging for all ePHI access. Without logs, you can't detect unauthorized access or breach activity.

How to Avoid It: Verify your telehealth platform has audit logging enabled. Periodically review logs for suspicious activity. Document your review process. Include audit logging in your vendor evaluation criteria.

Violation #7: Unvetted Subcontractors

The Problem: Telehealth vendor uses subcontractors without ensuring they have BAAs or meet security standards.

Why It's a Violation: Your BAA with the telehealth vendor should require them to hold subcontractors to HIPAA standards. If subcontractors have access to PHI without appropriate safeguards, this is your liability.

How to Avoid It: Require your BAA to include provisions for subcontractor management. Ask vendors for a list of subcontractors. Request attestations that subcontractors are bound by HIPAA safeguards.

Violation #8: No Incident Response Plan

The Problem: If a telehealth platform is breached, you have no documented process for responding, investigating, or notifying patients.

Why It's a Violation: The Breach Notification Rule requires notification within 60 days of discovery. Without a plan, you'll scramble and likely miss deadlines.

How to Avoid It: Develop a telehealth-specific incident response plan. Define roles (who leads response, who contacts patients, who notifies OCR). Establish a breach reporting timeline. Practice your response to a simulated breach annually.

Violation #9: No Security Risk Analysis for Telehealth

The Problem: Your Security Risk Analysis predates telehealth expansion and doesn't address telehealth-specific threats.

Why It's a Violation: HIPAA requires a current, comprehensive SRA. An outdated SRA that ignores your telehealth program is not compliant.

How to Avoid It: Update your SRA to specifically assess telehealth risks. Document threats (platform compromise, insecure provider locations, device security), vulnerabilities, and mitigations. Have an expert review your SRA if possible. Repeat every 1-2 years.

Violation #10: Inadequate Staff Training

The Problem: Providers and staff aren't trained on telehealth security best practices (secure workspaces, device security, recording consent, etc.).

Why It's a Violation: HIPAA requires security awareness training. If telehealth is a core part of your practice, staff must be trained on it.

How to Avoid It: Include telehealth security in your annual HIPAA training. Topics should cover platform best practices, device security, workspace requirements, recording consent, and incident reporting. Document attendance and completion. Provide role-specific training for clinicians vs. administrative staff.

Audit Yourself Before OCR Does Go through this list and honestly assess which violations your organization might have. If you find deficiencies, document a remediation plan with timelines and responsible parties. Self-remediation demonstrates good faith compliance and reduces OCR penalties if you're eventually audited. Don't wait for OCR contact to fix obvious problems.
