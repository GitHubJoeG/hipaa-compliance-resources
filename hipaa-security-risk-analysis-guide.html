What is a HIPAA Security Risk Analysis?

A HIPAA Security Risk Analysis (SRA)—also called a Security Risk Assessment—is a systematic, documented evaluation of all systems and processes that create, store, receive, maintain, or transmit electronic protected health information (ePHI). The SRA identifies potential threats and vulnerabilities to your ePHI, evaluates your current safeguards, rates the level of risk from identified vulnerabilities, and develops actionable remediation plans.

The SRA is required under 45 CFR 164.308(a)(1) of the HIPAA Security Rule and is the single most important compliance activity your organization can undertake. It serves as the foundation for all other HIPAA security compliance efforts—without a thorough, documented SRA, you cannot meaningfully implement the Security Rule's other requirements.

Why the SRA Matters Most OCR investigations consistently show that missing or inadequate Security Risk Analysis is the #1 violation finding. In fact, HHS states that the SRA is "the most important and critical step" in HIPAA compliance. This single deficiency has resulted in civil settlements in the millions of dollars.

The Regulatory Requirement

The HIPAA Security Rule specifically requires covered entities and business associates to "conduct an accurate and thorough assessment of the potential risks and vulnerabilities to the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of electronic protected health information held by the [organization]." This assessment must be comprehensive, organization-wide, and systematically documented.

The requirement is not to achieve perfect security or eliminate all risk. Rather, it's to understand what you have, what could go wrong, what protections you've implemented, and what gaps remain that need addressing.

Why is a Security Risk Analysis Required?

HIPAA requires the SRA because healthcare organizations handle some of the most sensitive personal information in existence. A breach of patient data can cause severe harm—identity theft, financial fraud, discrimination in insurance or employment, and psychological distress. The SRA ensures organizations systematically think through their security posture rather than implementing controls haphazardly.

The Risk Management Process

The SRA is part of a broader risk management cycle. Organizations must:

  • Identify what systems and information you have
  • Analyze threats and vulnerabilities
  • Evaluate current safeguards
  • Determine acceptable risk levels
  • Implement controls to manage identified risks
  • Monitor the effectiveness of your controls
  • Update your assessment when changes occur
Not Optional for Any Organization Some organizations mistakenly believe they're too small or too low-risk to require a formal SRA. HIPAA contains no size exemptions. If you're a covered entity or business associate handling ePHI electronically, the SRA is mandatory—regardless of whether you have 2 employees or 2,000.

Legal and Compliance Benefits

Beyond the regulatory requirement, a thorough SRA provides multiple benefits: it establishes a baseline for security posture, provides documented evidence of due diligence (important in litigation and regulatory investigations), identifies risks before they become breaches, prioritizes limited security resources on the highest-impact controls, demonstrates to patients and partners that you take security seriously, and provides board-level visibility into organizational risk.

The Complete SRA Process: Step-by-Step

A comprehensive Security Risk Analysis follows a structured methodology. While different frameworks use slightly different terminology, all follow this general progression:

The Six-Step SRA Framework (1) Asset Inventory, (2) Threat Identification, (3) Vulnerability Assessment, (4) Current Safeguard Evaluation, (5) Risk Determination, (6) Remediation Planning. Each step builds on the previous one and must be thoroughly documented.

Let's examine each step in detail.

Step 1: Create a Comprehensive Asset Inventory

You cannot protect what you don't know you have. The first and most critical SRA step is identifying every system, device, and process in your organization that creates, stores, receives, maintains, or transmits ePHI—whether locally, in the cloud, or hybrid.

What to Include in Your Asset Inventory

  • Clinical Systems: EHR/EMR systems, practice management software, imaging systems (PACS), laboratory information systems, radiology/ultrasound devices, monitoring equipment that connects to networks
  • Communication Systems: Email servers and clients, text messaging platforms, fax machines and servers, video conferencing systems, remote access tools, VPNs
  • Devices: Desktop computers, laptops, tablets, smartphones, printers, scanners, multi-function devices that may store ePHI
  • Storage Systems: Local file servers, network-attached storage (NAS), cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive), database servers
  • Backup and Disaster Recovery: Backup appliances, cloud backup services, tape backup systems, disaster recovery sites
  • Networking Infrastructure: Routers, switches, firewalls, VPN devices, wireless access points, network monitoring tools
  • Vendor Systems: Cloud-based vendors with access to your ePHI, telemedicine platforms, patient portal solutions, remote monitoring systems
  • Security Tools: Antivirus/malware solutions, intrusion detection systems, data loss prevention tools, authentication systems
  • Physical Assets: Server rooms, storage rooms containing paper records, office locations with PHI access, locked file cabinets

Asset Inventory Documentation

Your asset inventory should be organized in a spreadsheet or database documenting: asset name/description, asset type (server, workstation, software, etc.), location, owner/responsible party, system or software version, operating system, whether ePHI is stored/processed/transmitted, data classification level, criticality to operations, and any relevant notes.

Don't Forget About Shadow IT Many organizations discover during SRA that departments are using unapproved systems with ePHI. This might include shared Google Docs with patient information, personal email used for clinical communications, or personal devices accessing practice systems. While you may not approve of these uses, they must be identified and addressed in your SRA.

For Small Practices

Even a 2-3 person practice likely has 20-30 assets when you account for computers, printers, phones, cloud services, backup systems, and networking equipment. The asset inventory typically becomes the foundation document referenced throughout the SRA process.

Step 2: Identify Threats to ePHI

Once you've inventoried your assets, the next step is identifying potential threats—any event or circumstance that could exploit vulnerabilities and cause harm to your ePHI. Threats can be intentional or unintentional, internal or external, technical or physical.

Common Threats to Healthcare ePHI

  • Ransomware attacks: Malicious software encrypting files and demanding payment for decryption
  • Phishing and social engineering: Attackers tricking employees into revealing credentials or installing malware
  • Unauthorized access: Attackers gaining system access through stolen credentials, weak passwords, or security gaps
  • Insider threats: Employees or contractors inappropriately accessing or disclosing patient information
  • Device theft/loss: Laptops, phones, or portable drives containing unencrypted ePHI being lost or stolen
  • Misconfiguration: Cloud services, databases, or file sharing incorrectly configured to expose patient data
  • System vulnerabilities: Known and unpatched software vulnerabilities exploited by attackers
  • Denial of service: Attacks overwhelming systems and making them unavailable to legitimate users
  • Data interception: Network traffic containing ePHI being captured and read by unauthorized parties
  • Malware and viruses: Malicious software compromising system integrity and confidentiality
  • Natural disasters: Fire, flood, storms, or other events destroying or disrupting systems
  • Physical security breaches: Unauthorized access to server rooms, file rooms, or office spaces
  • Employee error: Accidental misconfiguration, misfiling, or data disclosure by staff
  • Vendor breaches: Third-party vendors or business associates being compromised

Threat Analysis by Asset Type

Your threat identification should be systematic. For each asset identified in Step 1, consider which threats could plausibly affect it. A laptop face different threats than a server; a cloud service faces different threats than a paper file room.

Tailor Threats to Your Environment While using standard threat lists is helpful, the best threat analysis is customized to your actual operational context. A coastal healthcare facility should include hurricane risk; an urban practice should address different threats than a rural one; a facility with extensive remote work faces different threats than one with all on-site staff.

Step 3: Identify Vulnerabilities and Weaknesses

A vulnerability is a weakness in your systems, policies, or processes that could be exploited by a threat. While threats are external events, vulnerabilities are internal weaknesses. A system may be vulnerable to malware infection if antivirus is not installed—the vulnerability is the missing antivirus, the threat is actual malware.

Common Vulnerabilities in Healthcare Settings

  • Weak or default passwords: Staff using weak passwords or vendors leaving default credentials unchanged
  • Unpatched systems: Operating systems, applications, and firmware with known vulnerabilities that haven't been updated
  • Lack of encryption: ePHI transmitted or stored without encryption protection
  • Shared credentials: Multiple users sharing login accounts rather than having individual user IDs
  • Missing authentication: Systems not requiring login credentials at all
  • Lack of access controls: No restrictions on who can access ePHI or what they can do with it
  • No audit logging: Systems not recording who accessed what data and when
  • Outdated software: Using software versions no longer receiving security updates
  • Unsecured networks: Wireless networks without strong encryption or guest networks with open access
  • Lack of backup testing: Having backups but never testing whether they can actually restore data
  • No access removal procedures: Terminated employees or contractors still having system access
  • Missing documentation: Poorly documented systems or processes creating confusion about how to operate them securely
  • Inadequate physical security: Server rooms or file rooms without locks, security cameras, or access controls
  • Vendor access not monitored: Business associates having access to systems but no monitoring of what they access

Vulnerability Assessment Methodology

Identifying vulnerabilities requires both technical and administrative assessment. Technical assessment might include vulnerability scanning tools, penetration testing, code review, or security audits. Administrative assessment involves reviewing policies, procedures, and configurations; interviewing staff about processes; and observing actual practices in the workplace.

The goal is to identify gaps between what your policies require and what's actually happening in practice. A common finding is that organizations have good policies on paper but inadequate enforcement in practice.

Step 4: Evaluate Current Safeguards

The fourth SRA step involves documenting the security controls and safeguards your organization has already implemented to protect ePHI. This is not about what you wish you had—it's about what you actually have in place today.

Safeguards to Evaluate

HIPAA's Security Rule organizes safeguards into three categories:

Administrative Safeguards

  • Security Risk Analysis process and documentation
  • Designated Security Officer responsible for security
  • Workforce security procedures (authorization, access management)
  • Information access management controls
  • Security awareness and training programs
  • Security incident procedures and response plan
  • Contingency planning (backup, disaster recovery, emergency mode operations)
  • Business Associate Agreements with vendors
  • Regular security control evaluation

Physical Safeguards

  • Facility access controls (visitor logs, ID badges, restricted areas)
  • Workstation security (locked screens, screen privacy, physical locks)
  • Workstation use policies (clean desk, appropriate use)
  • Device and media controls (secure storage, disposal procedures, inventory tracking)

Technical Safeguards

  • Access controls (unique user IDs, role-based access, passwords)
  • Audit controls and logging
  • Integrity controls (digital signatures, checksums)
  • Transmission security (encryption for data in transit)

Documenting Safeguards

For each safeguard, document what you've implemented: Does your practice have a documented security incident response plan? Which specific procedures does it include? Who is responsible? Have it been tested? How often? When was it last updated? This documentation creates the baseline for understanding your current security posture and identifying gaps.

Distinguish Between "Required" and "Addressable" The Security Rule classifies safeguards as either "Required" (must implement) or "Addressable" (must assess and implement if reasonable, or document why you chose an alternative). The distinction is crucial—"addressable" doesn't mean optional. You must still evaluate each addressable safeguard and document your decision.

Step 5: Determine Risk Levels

After identifying threats and vulnerabilities, and documenting current safeguards, you must analyze and determine the level of risk from each identified vulnerability. Risk determination involves evaluating likelihood (how probable is this threat?) and impact (how severe would a breach be?).

Risk Scoring Methodology

Most organizations use a simple framework for risk determination, combining likelihood and impact into an overall risk rating:

Likelihood: How probable is this threat to exploit this vulnerability? Consider factors like threat actors' motivation, technical capability required to exploit the vulnerability, number of people who could potentially cause harm, historical frequency of this type of threat in healthcare, and controls currently in place that make exploitation less likely.

Impact: How severe would a breach resulting from this vulnerability be? Consider factors like how many patients would be affected, types of information exposed (diagnoses may be more sensitive than appointment dates), legal/regulatory consequences, financial impact (breach notification costs, reputation damage), and patient harm potential.

Sample Risk Matrix

A common approach uses a 3x3 matrix:

Risk Level Likelihood Impact Example Vulnerabilities
Critical High High Unencrypted patient data accessible on shared network drive
High High/Medium Medium/High Missing patches on systems handling ePHI, no access controls on database
Medium Medium Medium Weak password policies, missing audit logs on non-critical systems
Low Low Low/Medium Outdated documentation, minor policy gaps

The goal is not mathematical precision but to differentiate critical risks requiring immediate attention from lower-priority issues that can be addressed over time within resource constraints.

Document Your Risk Decisions Your risk ratings must be justified and documented. During OCR investigations, auditors will review your risk determinations and want to understand why you rated something as low risk. Simply assigning numbers without explanation is insufficient.

Step 6: Create a Remediation Plan

The final SRA step is developing an actionable remediation plan that addresses identified risks. This is where your SRA transitions from analysis to action.

Remediation Plan Components

For each identified vulnerability, your remediation plan should specify:

  • Control or Action: What specifically will be done? (e.g., "Deploy multi-factor authentication," "Implement encryption on portable devices," "Develop incident response procedures")
  • Risk Addressed: Which vulnerability or risk does this control address?
  • Responsible Party: Who is accountable for implementation?
  • Timeline: When will this be completed? (Critical items should have near-term timelines; lower-risk items may have longer timelines)
  • Resources Required: What budget, software, or expertise is needed?
  • Status: Is this pending, in progress, or completed?

Prioritization Framework

Not all remediations can happen simultaneously. Prioritize based on risk level, ease of implementation, cost-effectiveness, and business impact. Address critical and high-risk items first. Quick wins (easy, inexpensive fixes with high security impact) can build momentum. Complex projects requiring significant investment might be phased over time.

Realistic Timelines

OCR doesn't expect perfect security immediately, but it expects reasonable timelines with demonstrated progress. A remediation plan that lists every action as "pending" without timelines or shows no progress after a year is problematic. Conversely, overly aggressive timelines that aren't met damage credibility.

Living Document Approach Your remediation plan should be a living document. As you complete remediations, mark them completed and update timelines. As new risks are identified between formal SRA updates, add them to the plan. This demonstrates that you're actively managing risk, not just checking a compliance box.

Following Up on Remediations

A critical step many organizations miss: verification that remediation actually solved the problem. If you plan to "implement access controls," actually verify that access controls were implemented correctly and are functioning as intended. A common OCR finding is that organizations documented remediation plans but never verified they were actually completed or effective.

Common SRA Mistakes to Avoid

Based on actual OCR investigations and enforcement actions, here are the most common SRA mistakes organizations make:

Generic or Template-Based Analysis

Using a generic SRA template without customization to your actual systems and environment. A good template provides structure, but you must populate it with your organization's specific assets, threats, vulnerabilities, and controls. An SRA that could apply to any organization is not a real risk analysis.

Incomplete Asset Inventory

Missing systems in the initial inventory. This often includes forgotten systems (older equipment still in use), vendor systems (cloud-based ePHI accessed through third parties), or employee-created systems (shadow IT). If a system isn't in your inventory, it won't be analyzed.

Inadequate Threat Identification

Only considering external threats and ignoring insider threats. Statistics show healthcare organizations face significant risk from employee actions—both intentional (data theft, snooping on celebrities) and unintentional (misconfiguration, human error). A complete threat analysis includes both.

Underestimating Vulnerability Impact

Rating vulnerabilities as low-risk when they actually pose significant threat. Unencrypted portable devices containing patient records might be rated low-risk based on "unlikely to be breached," but if a breach occurs, impact is catastrophic. Don't confuse likelihood with impact.

Documenting Ideal State Rather than Current State

Recording what your security policy says you should have, rather than what you actually have. An SRA that documents "all systems are kept current with patches" when you actually haven't patched systems in months is dishonest and provides no real value.

Over-Relying on Vendor Claims

Taking vendors' word that their systems are secure without independent verification. During vendor evaluation, organizations often accept vendor-provided security documentation at face value. Effective SRA involves independent assessment of vendor security, not just review of vendor marketing materials.

No Executive Involvement

Treating SRA as a compliance checkbox completed by IT with no involvement from executive leadership. The best SRAs have input from clinical leadership (who understand workflow and real-world risk), IT leadership (who understand technical controls), and executives (who can prioritize resources). A purely IT-focused SRA may miss important organizational context.

Inadequate Remediation Planning

Identifying risks but having no real plan to address them. "We know we have weak access controls" identified without timeline or ownership doesn't constitute a remediation plan. Good remediation plans are specific, actionable, and assigned to responsible parties with firm timelines.

Fire-and-Forget Documentation

Completing the SRA once and shelving it. HIPAA requires ongoing risk analysis. An SRA that was thorough but hasn't been updated in three years is no longer meeting the regulatory requirement. Updates should occur at least annually and whenever significant changes occur.

Inadequate Documentation**

Failing to document the methodology, assumptions, and decisions made during SRA. During OCR investigations, auditors want to understand not just what you found but how you conducted the analysis. Insufficient documentation makes it impossible to defend your process or demonstrate thoroughness.

No Board Awareness

SRA findings and remediation status not being communicated to the board. Board-level governance requires that risk management issues are escalated to the board. An organization where the board is unaware of significant security risks lacks the governance oversight HIPAA expects.

How Often Should You Conduct an SRA?

While HIPAA doesn't specify an exact frequency for conducting security risk analysis, the regulatory requirement is for "ongoing" risk analysis. In practice, this means both periodic comprehensive updates and continuous monitoring for changes.

Annual Comprehensive SRA

The industry standard and what OCR auditors expect is a comprehensive SRA at least annually. This involves reviewing your asset inventory, reassessing threats and vulnerabilities, updating your remediation status, and identifying any new risks. Annual updates keep your risk assessment current and demonstrate an ongoing commitment to risk management.

Interim Updates and Change Assessments

Beyond the annual SRA, you should update your risk analysis whenever significant changes occur:

  • New systems or technology: EHR upgrade, cloud service adoption, new medical devices connecting to your network
  • Changes to workflow: New telehealth capabilities, hybrid work arrangements, change in remote access patterns
  • Organizational changes: Mergers, acquisitions, office relocations, new departments handling ePHI
  • Staffing changes: Key security personnel departing, expansion into new specialties
  • Security incidents: Even incidents not qualifying as breaches (phishing attempts, near-misses) should trigger risk reassessment
  • Threat landscape changes: New vulnerabilities discovered in systems you use, new types of attacks emerging in healthcare
  • Regulatory changes: Updates to HIPAA rules or guidance that affect your analysis

Continuous Monitoring**

Between formal SRA updates, ongoing monitoring should identify emerging risks. This includes vulnerability scanning, security testing, user access reviews, and audit log analysis. Many organizations use automated tools to continuously monitor for security gaps rather than waiting for the annual SRA cycle.

Documentation of SRA Updates Keep records of when your SRA was conducted, what changes were made since the previous assessment, and what triggered interim updates. During OCR investigations, auditors review this documentation to understand your risk management process over time.

SRA Documentation Requirements

HIPAA doesn't prescribe an exact SRA format or template—it only requires that the assessment be "documented." However, effective documentation is critical because your SRA is primary evidence of compliance during regulatory investigations.

What Your SRA Documentation Should Include

  • Executive Summary: High-level overview of major findings, risk areas, and remediation priorities
  • Methodology: How the assessment was conducted, tools and techniques used, people involved, timeframe
  • Assumptions and Scope: What systems were included/excluded and why, any limitations of the assessment
  • Asset Inventory: Complete list of systems, devices, and processes containing or accessing ePHI
  • Threat Analysis: Identified threats with descriptions and context specific to your organization
  • Vulnerability Assessment: Identified vulnerabilities organized by system or by risk category, with impact analysis
  • Current Safeguard Documentation: Controls and safeguards currently in place with evidence of implementation
  • Risk Matrix and Ratings: How vulnerabilities were prioritized and scored, with justification for ratings
  • Remediation Plan: Actions to address identified risks, with responsible parties, timelines, and resource requirements
  • Sign-offs: Documentation that appropriate stakeholders (Security Officer, executive leadership, possibly board) reviewed and approved the SRA

Reasonable vs. Excessive Documentation

Your SRA doesn't need to be a thousand-page tome. Good SRA documentation is clear, organized, and sufficient to understand your risk management process. For a small practice, 30-50 pages of clear, well-organized documentation can be entirely adequate. For a larger organization, 100-200 pages might be appropriate. The key is that documentation is thorough enough that someone reading it would understand your security posture and risk management approach.

Storing Your SRA Documentation

Store SRA documentation securely—treat it as sensitive information because it documents your vulnerabilities. Consider encrypting your SRA files and limiting access to appropriate personnel. You don't want detailed vulnerability information to become a roadmap for attackers if accessed improperly.

SRA Updates and Versioning

When you update your SRA, keep previous versions for historical reference. This allows you to demonstrate the evolution of your risk management over time and show how you've addressed previously identified vulnerabilities. Most organizations maintain SRA versions from the past 3-5 years.

SRA Connection to Meaningful Use and MIPS

For organizations participating in Medicare's Meaningful Use program (now called the Quality Payment Program, or QPP with Merit-based Incentive Payment System—MIPS), Security Risk Analysis has additional significance beyond basic HIPAA compliance.

Meaningful Use and Security Risk Analysis

Meaningful Use criteria explicitly require conducting and documenting a security risk analysis. This isn't a separate activity from your HIPAA SRA—it's the same assessment, but it's assessed as part of your meaningful use attestation. Failing to conduct an SRA could result in losing meaningful use incentive payments or facing recovery of previously received payments.

MIPS and Security Risk Management

Under MIPS, the Improvement Activities category includes security risk management requirements. Clinicians and practices can earn credit for conducting security risk analysis, keeping security software current, and implementing security safeguards. Documenting your comprehensive SRA process can support MIPS reporting requirements.

EHR Certification and SRA

EHR systems must meet certification criteria including security requirements. Your use of a certified EHR doesn't replace the need for organizational SRA—certification confirms the vendor's security controls, but your organization's overall risk analysis must assess your entire environment.

DIY vs. Consultant vs. Software Platform Approaches

Organizations have several options for conducting their SRA. Each approach has benefits and limitations.

DIY Approach (Internal Resources)

Best for: Organizations with capable IT staff, IT security knowledge, and time to dedicate to the project.

Advantages: Lower cost, deep knowledge of your systems, process customized to your environment, complete control over methodology and scope.

Challenges: Time-intensive (a thorough SRA can take weeks or months), requires significant expertise, difficult to maintain objectivity when evaluating your own systems, potential blind spots when analyzing systems you manage, challenge defending methodology to auditors if it's internal-only.

Cost: Staff time only, typically $5,000-$20,000 in labor depending on organization size and complexity.

Consultant Approach (External Experts)

Best for: Organizations lacking IT security expertise, wanting independent assessment, or with complex environments requiring specialized knowledge.

Advantages: External expertise and objectivity, consultant brings best practices and industry experience, thorough assessment less likely to have blind spots, defensible methodology should OCR question your assessment, can provide recommendations beyond just risk analysis.

Challenges: High cost, consultant needs time to understand your systems (less familiarity than internal staff), finding qualified, reputable healthcare security consultants, depends on consultant's quality and integrity.

Cost: $5,000-$50,000+ depending on organization size, complexity, and consultant rates. Larger organizations or those with complex multi-location environments can easily exceed $50,000.

Software Platform Approach (Automated/Guided Tools)

Best for: Organizations wanting structured methodology, documented process, and ability to update assessments regularly. Increasingly popular option combining some efficiency of DIY with structured methodology of consulting.

Advantages: Structured, proven methodology guides you through all necessary steps, platforms like Medcurity provide templates, documentation storage, and compliance tracking, can be updated easily as risks change, more cost-effective than full consulting, provides audit trail of assessment process, continuing platform use enables ongoing risk management not just one-time assessment.

Challenges: Platform-guided assessment may feel generic, though good platforms customize to your specific systems, requires internal time investment to use platform effectively, depends on platform vendor's continued operations and support.

Cost: $200-$500/month typically for organizations up to 100-200 employees, or enterprise pricing for larger organizations. Over 2-3 years, can be comparable to a one-time consultant engagement but with continuing functionality.

Hybrid Approach

Many organizations combine approaches: use a consultant for initial comprehensive assessment, then use a software platform for annual updates and ongoing management. This leverages consultant expertise to establish a strong foundation while reducing ongoing costs and enabling more frequent updates.

Regardless of Approach: Documentation is Key Whether you conduct SRA internally, hire a consultant, or use a software platform, thorough documentation is essential. OCR will review your SRA methodology and reasoning. Ensure whatever approach you choose produces clear, comprehensive documentation of your assessment and findings.

Real Enforcement Examples: What Happens Without Proper SRA

Abstract compliance requirements become very concrete when examining real OCR enforcement actions. Here are actual cases where inadequate or missing Security Risk Analysis led to significant penalties:

Case 1: Dental Practice - $2.1 Million Settlement (2021)

A dental practice notified HHS of a breach affecting 45,000 patients when a contractor stored patient lists in an unsecured cloud storage. Investigation revealed the organization had never conducted a comprehensive security risk analysis, had no inventory of systems containing ePHI, had no documented risk management process, and lacked basic controls like access restrictions and audit logging.

Key Finding: The organization documented their general security policies but never conducted the systematic analysis required to identify vulnerabilities in their actual environment. The breach might have been prevented if they'd identified the unsecured cloud storage practice during a proper SRA.

Case 2: Healthcare Network - $1.55 Million Penalty + $2 Million Corrective Action Plan (2023)

A healthcare network suffered a breach exposing over 100,000 patient records. OCR's investigation found that the organization had not conducted a proper risk analysis in over 5 years, had not updated their risk assessment following new system implementations, had not evaluated threats from recent attack types (ransomware, specifically), and had not documented remediation status for previously identified vulnerabilities.

Key Finding: Organizations must keep their risk analysis current. A SRA that's several years old doesn't satisfy the "ongoing" risk analysis requirement. The organization failed to adapt their risk assessment to evolving threats.

Case 3: Small Physician Practice - $167,000 Penalty (2022)

A small practice (7 physicians) experienced a ransomware attack encrypting their EHR and patient files. Recovery took three weeks. OCR investigation discovered that while the practice had conducted an SRA five years earlier, it had never updated it. The assessment didn't identify the vulnerability (unpatched server) that attackers exploited. Additionally, the practice had not documented any remediation plan addressing the server patching vulnerability.

Key Finding: Even small organizations cannot claim SRA compliance if their assessment is outdated. The vulnerability existed, was identified in prior vulnerability scans, but no remediation had been implemented or tracked.

Case 4: Clinic Network - $2.67 Million Settlement + 5-Year Monitoring (2023)

A multi-clinic healthcare network notified HHS of a breach involving email compromise affecting 2.4 million patients. OCR investigation found extensive violations including no documented security risk analysis (despite four years of operation), no asset inventory of systems containing ePHI, no formal threat assessment, no vulnerability management program, and no documented safeguard evaluation. The organization had no evidence of risk-based decision-making about security controls.

Key Finding: This case illustrates that organizations cannot simply claim they're compliant without the documentation to back it up. The practice couldn't produce evidence of the SRA process even being initiated.

Case 5: Hospital - $6.8 Million Penalty (2023)

The largest healthcare organization fine by OCR to date involved a hospital system where vulnerability assessment was inadequate. Their SRA process did not systematically identify vulnerabilities, and when known vulnerabilities were discovered (unpatched systems, default credentials, misconfigured database), they were not documented in the risk analysis and therefore not included in remediation planning.

Key Finding: Even large organizations with substantial security budgets can face massive penalties if their risk analysis process is inadequate. The issue wasn't necessarily that vulnerabilities existed—it was that they weren't properly identified, analyzed, and remediated through a documented process.

Common Patterns in Enforcement Cases

  • No documented SRA or outdated SRA (more than a few years old)
  • SRA exists but is generic template with little customization to actual systems
  • Identified risks in SRA without documented remediation plans or status tracking
  • Vulnerabilities known to exist but not identified in the SRA process
  • SRA conducted but findings not communicated to executive leadership or board
  • No evidence that SRA findings actually influenced security decisions
The Pattern: Process, Documentation, and Action OCR penalties don't just reflect the existence of vulnerabilities (nearly all organizations have some). Penalties reflect failures in three areas: (1) having a documented, methodical process for identifying risks, (2) maintaining clear documentation of that process and findings, and (3) actually taking action to remediate identified risks. An organization can have vulnerabilities but satisfy HIPAA requirements if they have evidence of systematic risk management.

Frequently Asked Questions About Security Risk Analysis

Can we use a HIPAA SRA template from the internet? ▼

Yes, using templates is fine and actually recommended—they provide structure and ensure you don't miss key components. However, the template must be customized to your specific organization. A good template guides your process, but you must populate it with your actual assets, threats, vulnerabilities, and safeguards. The final SRA should be unique to your organization, reflecting your actual technology, workflows, and risk environment.

What if we use a certified EHR system? Does that reduce our SRA requirements? ▼

No. EHR certification confirms the vendor's security controls, but you still must conduct your own organizational risk analysis. EHR certification means the vendor has met certain security standards, but your SRA must evaluate your entire environment, including how the EHR is configured and used in your organization, what other systems connect to it, how you manage access and backups, and your broader security infrastructure.

Do we need to conduct penetration testing as part of our SRA? ▼

Not necessarily as a formal requirement, but penetration testing (having ethical hackers attempt to breach your systems) is an excellent vulnerability assessment technique that many organizations include. Whether to include penetration testing depends on your risk tolerance, available budget, and complexity of your environment. A basic SRA can be adequate without penetration testing, but adding it strengthens your assessment.

How much detail should our asset inventory include? ▼

Your asset inventory should be detailed enough to understand your environment and useful for ongoing asset management. For each asset, document the name, type, location, owner, operating system/version, whether ePHI is processed/stored/transmitted, and any relevant security notes. The inventory should be practical and maintainable—detailed enough to be useful for compliance and security management, but not so granular that it becomes unmanageable to maintain.

Should we include paper records in our Security Risk Analysis? ▼

The Security Risk Analysis applies specifically to electronic protected health information (ePHI). However, your comprehensive risk management should address all PHI, including paper records and oral communication. Some organizations conduct a separate Privacy Risk Analysis for physical/paper PHI. At minimum, your SRA should identify systems or processes where ePHI is printed or converted to paper, which falls under ePHI scope.

How do we address risks with extremely high costs to remediate? ▼

HIPAA's Security Rule permits risk-based decision-making. If a remediation is cost-prohibitive, you can document why you determined the risk is acceptable at current levels, explain any alternative controls you've implemented, and plan phased remediation if possible. The key is documenting your decision-making. However, high cost doesn't automatically justify accepting high risk—OCR will evaluate whether your risk acceptance was reasonable.

About the Author

Joe Gellatly is the CEO of Medcurity, a HIPAA compliance platform used by hundreds of healthcare organizations to manage their Security Risk Analysis and broader compliance programs. With years of experience helping practices and health systems navigate HIPAA's complex requirements, Joe is passionate about making Security Risk Analysis practical and accessible rather than overwhelming.

Joe regularly works with healthcare organizations of all sizes—from 2-person practices to large health systems—to build comprehensive, defensible risk management processes. His focus is always on the core principle: systematic identification and management of risks protecting patient data.
